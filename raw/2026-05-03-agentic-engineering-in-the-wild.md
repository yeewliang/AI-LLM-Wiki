## Real-world stories from early-adopting Singapore government teams experimenting with and delivering with AI

In my [previous article](https://sausheong.com/from-vibe-coding-to-agentic-engineering-1ca3ca72b5ac), I laid out a framework for understanding the shift from vibe coding to agentic engineering. I wrote about the bottleneck inversion, about the role of the engineer being redefined, about team structures shrinking and the boundaries between roles dissolving. I argued that 1 person with AI tools could reach proof-of-concept before any engineering resource is engaged, and that this changes everything we assume about team composition, project economics, and who gets to build things.

Those were arguments and what follows are some real-world stories of how early adopter GovTech Singapore teams have been incorporating AI tools in their development processes.

Over the past couple of weeks, I sat down with several people across a few of my teams at GovTech Singapore. I interviewed a number of team mates, including software engineers, product managers, data scientists and so on, to understand what AI-assisted coding actually looks like in practice. Not the strategy document version, not the 5-level framework in the abstract, but the real, messy, honest version as lived by the people doing the work.

What I got was pretty fascinating. In some cases, the numbers were so dramatic that I had to double-check them. But these aren’t projections or thought experiments. These are things that have already happened, with real users, real production systems, and real measurable outcomes.

So I thought I’d share their stories.

## The smallest possible team

“I am a software engineer working in the anti-scam team,” he began. “I’ll be upfront. I have really limited Android development experience. Like, pretty limited. But after our hackathon, something clicked, and within 2 weeks I personally delivered 2 fully functional projects as the sole developer on both.”

“Wait, 2 projects in 2 weeks?” I asked. “What were they?”

“The first one is an Android app with an on-device LLM that uses event notification listeners to scan messages for scams in real time,” he said. “The team was just one PM, 1 data scientist, and me.”

He paused.

“The second is an AI-powered autonomous agent that automates the end-to-end process of detecting, assessing, and disrupting scam enablers across online platforms. Operators can provide instructions in plain English to direct the agent’s investigation workflow. That team was even smaller. 1 PM and me.”

“So you built an Android app despite having limited Android experience, and an autonomous AI agent, both in 2 weeks,” I said. “What would this have looked like before?”

“Previously, these 2 projects would realistically require 2 to 3 specialised engineers. An Android developer, a software engineer, a frontend engineer. And it would likely take more than 2 to 3 months to reach the same stage,” he explained. “With Claude Code, 1 engineer delivered both in 2 weeks. With extra time to make a nice video, I might add.”

“Those are pretty staggering numbers,” I said.

“Right. If you do the maths, the old way would be 2 to 3 engineers times 8 to 12 weeks, which is 16 to 36 person-weeks. What I did was 1 engineer times 2 weeks. 2 person-weeks,” he said. “That’s conservatively an 8 to 12x reduction in engineering effort. What used to require a small team and a quarter now takes 1 person and a sprint.”

This is the *solo-to-scale model* I described in my previous article, playing out in real time. A single person with AI tools reaching proof-of-concept on not 1 but 2 products, before any traditional team could have been assembled, scoped, and kicked off. The cost of experimentation drops from months of team commitment to days of individual effort.

But what he said next surprised me more than the numbers.

He leaned forward. “But here’s the thing that I think is even more interesting. It’s not just about me. It’s reshaping the work of every non-engineer on the team too.”

“What do you mean by that?” I asked.

“Our PMs can now code simple features and verify the changes themselves,” he said. “Our data scientists use Claude to troubleshoot deep model issues. 1 of our PMs is also acting as a data scientist for another project, because of Claude. Another PM set up a workflow to write project documentation and prepare demo slides. PMs and devs use it for market research. PMs use Figma prompting and Stitch with MCP to design UI with Claude.”

“So the boundaries between roles are dissolving?” I asked.

“To a certain extent, yes. The cross-domain work is not as difficult as it used to be,” he said. “But only to a certain extent. I want to be honest about that. There are still limits. But the floor has risen dramatically. People who couldn’t build things before can now build things. And people who could build things before can now build a lot more, a lot faster.”

That’s pretty fascinating. In the previous article, I wrote about how the boundaries between roles were becoming less like walls and more like low fences you can step over when you need to. Here was someone describing that exact dynamic, but with a useful nuance. The fences are lower, not gone. The PM who codes a feature still benefits from having an engineer review it. The data scientist who builds a full-stack app still needs to understand the architectural decisions he’s making. What changes is that the cost of crossing into adjacent territory has dropped dramatically, not that the territory no longer exists.

“That’s quite something,” I said. “The idea that a PM can code features, verify changes, and essentially function as a data scientist on another project. That would have sounded absurd a couple of years ago.”

## The data scientist who built a platform

“I am a data scientist in the Government Technology Office,” he said. “No software engineering background at all. And I built what is now a core part of our go-to-market strategy for vision and multimodal AI.”

“That’s a pretty bold claim,” I said. “Tell me the backstory.”

“So last year, engineers and PMs were telling me it would take a few sprints to get a working prototype. That was too slow,” he recalled. “I needed agencies to see what multimodal AI could do, not hear about it on slides. So I collapsed the stack and built it myself using Claude Code.”

He continued, “It started in July last year with 9 basic showcases. It went slowly for the first couple of months as I was in another practice area and doing this as more of a personal project. But I persisted and it organically grew into what’s now part of our platform’s go-to-market strategy.”

This is exactly the scenario I described as the *bottleneck inversion*. The engineering work was no longer the long pole in the tent. The constraint was the traditional process of waiting for sprints to free up, for engineering capacity to become available, for the queue to clear. He didn’t wait. He built it himself.

In the previous article, I wrote that *“the organisations that will move fastest aren’t those with the best engineering teams. They’re those with the shortest distance between a decision and an action.”* Here was a data scientist who decided the distance was too long, and simply closed it.

“What does it look like now?” I asked.

“Just 2 data scientists, myself and another data scientist, no software engineers. All development done with Claude Code,” he said. “We’ve built over 30 showcases, which are essentially mini full-stack apps showing agencies how to leverage our platform’s capabilities, plus 12 capability playgrounds. Several of them have converted or are converting to actual customers. We have over 150 users across more than 20 agencies, and we’ve done very limited marketing for it so far.”

“30+ full-stack apps built by 2 data scientists with no software engineering background,” I said. “How does that even work?”

“Well, what used to take 5 to 7 days per showcase now takes 1 to 2 days, thanks to a monorepo with reusable patterns that Claude Code follows,” he explained. “A traditional team of 3 to 4 engineers plus 1 PM would need several sprints to reach the same point.”

The monorepo detail is worth pausing on. This is effectively platform thinking applied at the team level. The reusable patterns act as guardrails, the kind of “fast but safe path” I described in the platforms section of the previous article. Claude Code doesn’t just generate code from scratch each time. It follows established conventions, which means the output is consistent, reviewable, and maintainable. The platform becomes the curriculum.

“The numbers are pretty eye-opening,” I said. “For a single showcase, the traditional approach would be 2 engineers plus 1 PM over 2 sprints, about 12 person-weeks. With Claude Code, it’s 1 data scientist over 2 days, roughly 0.4 person-weeks. That’s about a 30x multiplier.”

“And for the overall studio?” I asked.

“If you look at the whole thing, 30+ showcases, 12 playgrounds, all full-stack with Next.js 15 and Python with FastAPI, a traditional approach would be 4 engineers plus 1 PM over 12 to 18 months, which is 60 to 90 person-months,” he said. “What we actually did was 2 data scientists, part-time, in about 4 person-months. That’s a 15 to 22x multiplier.”

“You mentioned your colleague the other data scientist,” I said. “What’s her story?”

“She had zero web development experience. Zero,” he said firmly. “But now she is now independently delivering full-stack showcases. The monorepo plus Claude Code essentially turned our codebase into a curriculum. She could look at existing patterns, prompt Claude Code with what she wanted, and it would follow the conventions already established. It’s almost like having a very patient, very knowledgeable senior engineer sitting next to you at all times.”

That last line struck me. In the previous article, I discussed how AI-native juniors carry potential advantages that are easy to underestimate. His colleague isn’t a junior engineer. She’s a junior data scientist. But the dynamic is the same. Someone without traditional engineering training, picking up the tools, learning by pattern-matching against a well-structured codebase, and delivering production-quality output. The monorepo acted as the structured mentoring environment I argued we needed to create deliberately.

“Is it just you and your colleague, or are others using it too?” I asked.

“Oh, it’s spread far beyond us,” he said. “Our PM used Claude to basically vibecode up what he thought was missing from 1 of our products. He was doing this over the December holidays, on his own initiative, on his own time, and it’s now actively used in his client engagements.” He went on. “1 of our UX designers uses Claude Code quite heavily for his UX work and also codes up plugins for Figma so he can quickly improve on his design iterations. Our engineering manager uses Claude to make his slides nowadays. They come across as a lot more polished compared to a typical plain white background slide.”

A PM vibecoding over the holidays. A UX designer building Figma plugins. An engineering manager polishing his presentations. This is the democratisation of building capability I described in the strategy, happening organically, without formal sanction and without a programme to drive it. Just people discovering they could build things and doing it.

And this is also where I felt that same knot in my stomach I wrote about previously. These tools are spreading faster than governance can keep up. That PM’s holiday project is now in client engagements. Did it get a security review? Does anyone besides him know how it works? The capability is extraordinary, but without guardrails it creates risk at exactly the speed it creates value.

He smiled. “Across the board in our multimodal AI team, we use it for all shapes and forms of purposes. Honestly, without it we would be a lot slower and it would be a very different level of motivation and enthusiasm. Using Claude Code has really lifted us all. These days we are very excited about what’s possible because most of it is just a prompt away.”

“That last point is interesting,” I said. “The motivation angle. It’s not just about productivity, is it?”

“Not at all,” he said. “When you can see your idea come to life in a day instead of waiting weeks for a sprint to free up, it changes how you think about what’s possible. You become more ambitious. You try more things. Some of them don’t work out, but the cost of trying is so low that it doesn’t matter. The enthusiasm is real.”

This might be the least quantifiable observation, and possibly the most important one. In the previous article, I focused heavily on productivity multipliers, governance frameworks, and organisational structures. All these are necessary. But what I heard in these conversations was something harder to capture in a strategy document. When the distance between idea and working thing shrinks to nearly nothing, people don’t just work faster. Instead, they think differently. They become more ambitious. They try things they wouldn’t have tried before, because the cost of failure is measured in hours rather than months.

The portfolio approach I recommended in the previous article, try many initiatives and back the ones that work, doesn’t just make strategic sense. It becomes the natural behaviour of teams when the tools make experimentation cheap.

## The engineer who became an entire team

“I am a software engineer in the Government Technology Office,” he said, “and I built a production-grade, enterprise-scale AI system that’s actively used across over 150 projects. Including Singpass, GovWallet, TradeNet, and anti-scam systems, among many others.” He paused. “By myself.”

“That’s not a prototype or a demo?” I asked.

“No,” he said flatly. “This is *Prelude*. An AI-powered CI/CD code review agent. When a developer opens a merge request, Prelude spins up an isolated sandbox container with Claude Code inside, analyses the code changeset against government code classification policies, generates structured review comments with confidence scoring, deduplicates comments to avoid noise, and posts comments directly on the merge request. It also records feedback for continuous improvement.”

He continued, “On top of that, it runs a real-time analytics dashboard backed by ClickHouse and exposes a REST API deployed in high-availability mode with 2 instances.”

Readers of the previous article will recognise Prelude. I mentioned it in the section on code review evolution as our in-house AI code review tool. What I didn’t fully convey was the scale of what a single engineer had built. So let me lay it out.

“That sounds like the kind of system that would normally have a dedicated team,” I said.

“That’s exactly the point,” he said. “The system spans 6 independently deployable packages requiring expertise across multiple specialisations simultaneously.” He started counting them off. “Backend and API work. Express.js, webhook handling, job authentication, graceful shutdown. AI and ML engineering. 4 specialised prompts, schema validation on LLM output, confidence scoring, deduplication, fallback routing between different AI providers. Frontend and data visualisation. A React 19 dashboard with over 10 pages covering compliance views, error tracking, feedback loops, cost metrics, histograms. Data engineering. ClickHouse time-series schema, OpenTelemetry instrumentation, distributed tracing. DevOps and infrastructure. Docker multi-stage builds, GitLab CI/CD pipelines, sandbox isolation. Security and compliance. Government code classification enforcement, IP filtering across 11+ CIDR blocks, token management, webhook authentication.”

“That’s a lot of hats you’re wearing,” I said.

“It is,” he agreed. “A conservative staffing estimate without Claude Code would be 3 to 5 engineers across backend, frontend, AI/ML, and DevOps, taking 6 to 12 months to reach the same functional state.”

6 technical domains. 1 person. This is the team structure shift I predicted in the previous article, taken to its logical extreme. I wrote that *“if 1 person with AI can reach proof-of-concept before any engineering resource is engaged, everything we assume about team composition, vendor engagement and project economics needs re-examination.”* Prelude isn’t a proof-of-concept. It’s a production system serving over 150 government projects. And it was built by 1 person.

“And you did it alone,” I said. “How do the commit numbers tell that story?”

“The git history is pretty revealing,” he said. “In January 2026 alone, when heavy Claude Code adoption began, I hit 256 commits and 94 merge requests in a single month. The monthly baseline before that was around 60 to 70 commits. That’s roughly a 3.5 to 4x increase in commit velocity by a single developer.”

“And this wasn’t your only project?” I asked.

“No, that’s the other thing,” he said. “I was concurrently contributing to 6+ other projects during this period. So the effective multiplier on Prelude alone is 3 to 5x, but when you account for the parallel work, it’s considerably higher.”

“So let me get this straight,” I said. “You, a single engineer, built and maintained an enterprise-grade AI system now serving over 150 government projects, spanning 6 different technical domains, while simultaneously delivering 6 other projects. And Claude Code was being used for everything from multi-file refactoring to architecture design to ClickHouse SQL queries to React dashboard development.”

“Yes,” he said. “And 92% of my Claude Code sessions fully or mostly achieved their goal. It’s not magic. You still need to know what you’re doing, you still need to make architectural decisions, you still need to review what comes out. But the execution speed is just in a completely different league.”

That last point is critical, and it connects directly to the trust and abstraction concerns I raised in the previous article. He didn’t say the AI did the thinking for him. He said the AI did the execution. He still made the architectural decisions. He still understood the government security classification policies. He still knew why ClickHouse was the right choice for time-series data. Claude Code didn’t replace his expertise. It amplified it.

This is *supervisory engineering* in action. Not the theoretical version I described in the strategy, but the real thing. Directing agents, evaluating their output, making judgment calls about what to delegate and what to decide yourself. And doing it across 6 technical domains simultaneously, which maps uncomfortably well to my warning about the burnout risk of managing multiple agents in parallel.

## What their stories tell us

I sat with these stories for a while before writing this up, because the numbers are frankly a bit hard to believe. 8 to 12x. 15 to 22x. 30x. A data scientist with no web development experience independently delivering full-stack applications. A PM vibecoding features over the Christmas holidays. A single engineer maintaining a production system that would normally need a team of 5.

If someone had told me this 5 years ago, I would have smiled politely and changed the subject. But now, nearly every theme I outlined in the previous article showed up in these conversations, often in ways I hadn’t anticipated.

The bottleneck inversion is real. The data scientist didn’t wait for engineering capacity. He built it himself because the traditional process was too slow. The constraint wasn’t the building. It was the waiting.

The solo-to-scale model works. The software engineer took 2 products from zero to functional in 2 weeks, alone. Another built an enterprise system that would normally need a team of 5. These aren’t startups. This is government.

The role boundaries are shifting. PMs are coding features. Data scientists are shipping full-stack apps. UX designers are building Figma plugins. The fences between roles are lower than I expected them to be, even accounting for my own predictions.

Platforms are the enabling layer. The monorepo that turned a codebase into a curriculum. The Agent Prime Directives providing shared context. These are the “fast but safe paths” I argued for, and they’re working.

Supervisory engineering is the core skill. Every person I spoke to was clear that the AI didn’t do the thinking. It did the execution. The human value was in the decisions, the architecture, the judgment about what to build and whether the output was trustworthy. That’s the new core competency, and it’s exactly the one I said we need to start deliberately cultivating.

==And the motivation effect is real.== This one I hadn’t expected to be so pronounced. When the cost of trying something drops to nearly zero, people don’t just do more of the same. They think bigger. They try things they wouldn’t have tried before. The enthusiasm wasn’t performed. It was palpable in every conversation.

But there are hard questions embedded in the good news. When a PM builds a tool over the holidays that’s now in client engagements, who reviewed it? When a single engineer is responsible for a production system spanning 6 domains, what happens when that person is unavailable? When non-engineers are shipping full-stack apps, how do we ensure those apps meet government security and compliance standards?

These are the governance questions I raised in the strategy. And the stories make them more urgent, not less because these tools aren’t spreading at the pace of a carefully managed rollout. They’re spreading at the pace of people discovering they can build things. Governance needs to keep up.

I want to be clear about what this is and what it isn’t. Nobody I spoke to said the AI does the thinking for them. The software engineer still needed to understand what an on-device LLM is and how event notification listeners work. The data scientist still had to make architectural decisions about the monorepo. The platform engineer still needed to understand government security classification policies and ClickHouse schemas. Claude Code didn’t replace the expertise. It amplified it.

And that might be the most important insight of all. The previous article was about the theory of what’s changing. These stories are the proof. The shift from vibe coding to agentic engineering isn’t coming because it’s already here. The question is no longer whether this changes how we build software. It’s whether we can build the governance, the platforms, and the organisational structures fast enough to keep up with the people who are already building the future.

That’s the work ahead.
