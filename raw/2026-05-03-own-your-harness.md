In the past couple of months, I’ve written a few articles on agent harnesses, and also why everyone seems to be focused on it. The TL;DR is that it’s the new frontier. Yes, they’re still battling it out at the model front (e.g. DeepSeek just released v4, OpenAI released GPT-5.5, Anthropic is scaring everyone shitless with Mythos) but the real battle is going to be at the harness.

OpenAI has moved beyond models and is building a full agent system. It now offers workspace agents that can execute multi-step tasks across tools and retain context, an Agents SDK that standardises how tools, memory, and orchestration work, and Frontier to manage these agents at scale. Anthropic is taking a parallel path. Instead of one general-purpose harness, it is creating domain-specific environments like Claude Code, Claude Cowork, and Claude Design, each built on the same model but tuned for a specific type of work.

Microsoft and Google are pushing this further into enterprise infrastructure. Microsoft is turning Copilot into a system layer, with Agent 365 adding identity, governance, orchestration, and lifecycle management so agents become first-class entities. Google made the shift explicit at Cloud Next 2026 with the Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform, positioning agents as the layer that connects data, tools, and execution across the organisation. And then there is xAI. Instead of competing purely at the model layer, it is moving to acquire Cursor, an AI-native coding environment, effectively buying workflow, distribution, and a developer execution loop. In other words, the harness.

The pattern is now unmistakable. Across every major player, the model is becoming just one component. The real product is the harness, the system that owns context, tools, execution, identity, and integration, and that is where the competition is shifting.

I’ve also been asked a few times now, how complex can the harness be? It’s just some software layer over the model, right? The important part is the model, everything else is just fluff. Even Anthropic said so. Boris Cherny, who built Claude Code, called it “the thinnest possible wrapper over the model” on the Latent Space podcast in May 2025, adding that “all the secret sauce, it’s all in the model.” In that case, let’s just buy a harness from someone and be done with it. We don’t want to tie down our resources on something that’s a commodity and a thin wrapper over the model.

I think many people still don’t understand how complex the harness can be and how much the different strategies matter. It’s the difference between building genuinely powerful agents and mediocre ones that just lean on the model’s capabilities. Don’t get me wrong, I’m not saying models are not important. Rather I’m saying the harness is going to be the differentiator. As Shangru put it aptly in another article, the model is the engine, but the harness is the vehicle. Both are important but unless you have a tonne of money you can’t own your model, so you should try to own your harness. Else you’re just a passenger.

In this article, I want to dive deeper into 2 of the most popular agents around, Claude Code and OpenClaw. As fate would have it, Anthropic accidentally leaked their Claude Code source code, and there’s plenty of repos around that we can peek into the innards of the most popular coding agent (as of now). And of course OpenClaw code is open source and readily available for me to poke around. Their codebases are pretty huge so I will focus on just one part, the compaction strategy.

## What compaction actually is

So … what is compaction?

When an agent is in a long conversation with its model, the conversation history grows on every turn. Every tool call, every file read, every error message, every bit of model output gets stitched back into the prompt sent on the next turn. Models have a context window. For modern frontier models that’s usually 200,000 tokens, sometimes a million if you pay for the privilege. Eventually you hit the wall. Compaction is the umbrella term for everything an agent does to keep the conversation from spilling over the edge.

You can do it cheaply by dropping old tool results or trimming a noisy bash log. You can do it expensively by calling the model again to summarise the entire conversation. You can do it before there’s a problem (predictive) or after the wall is in sight (reactive). You can replace history with a summary, or write important facts to a persistent file the agent can search later. Most real agents do several of these at once, and the order, the thresholds, the prompts, the way each technique interacts with the prompt cache, all of it adds up to a stack with more design choices than you’d think.

Claude Code and OpenClaw both have such a stack. They are layered, defensive, and pretty opinionated. They also disagree about almost everything.

## Two opposite strategies

The first and biggest difference is when each system decides to compact.

Claude Code is reactive. It lets the conversation grow until projected token usage approaches the context window, and only then does it summarise. The trigger formula sits in `autoCompact.ts`. Auto-compaction fires when the running token count is within 13,000 tokens of the effective context window, and manual `/compact` blocks at 3,000. There's also a true reactive fallback that catches the API's `prompt_too_long` 413 error if everything else somehow misses the threshold. The model is trusted to keep going right up to the wall.

OpenClaw is preemptive. Before every single LLM call, it estimates the total token cost of the next request, multiplies by a 1.2 safety margin, and asks itself whether the call will fit. If it won’t, it routes to the cheapest viable strategy. That might be just truncating an oversized tool result (`truncate_tool_results_only`), or a full summarise (`compact_only`), or both (`compact_then_truncate`). On top of that there's a `transformContext` guard that throws "Context overflow" outright if a request projects more than 90% of the window.

These are very different strategies. Claude Code assumes the model will usually finish its work before hitting the limit, so it lets the context run hot and only pays the summarisation cost when forced. OpenClaw assumes that estimating tokens before every call (essentially zero cost) is worth doing to avoid surprises and to escalate gracefully through cheaper options before reaching the expensive one.

Neither is obviously correct. Reactive is simpler and faster on the happy path. Preemptive is more defensive and more flexible when things get weird (multiple long tool results in a row, an unexpectedly bloated file read, a model that produces a much longer reply than usual). The fact that two well-engineered agents land on opposite ends of this spectrum tells you something about how unsettled this design space still is.

## The cheap stuff that runs every turn

Both agents have a layer of tricks that runs constantly and avoids LLM calls entirely. This is the part people don’t usually think about when they hear “compaction”.

Claude Code has the most ‘cheating’ trick of the bunch, and it’s only available because Anthropic owns its own API. There’s a beta feature called `cache_edits` that lets the client tell the API server to delete specific tool results from the cached prompt. The local message list stays intact, but the cached prefix gets surgically edited, and stale tool results stop costing tokens without invalidating the prompt cache. For a 200K-token cached prefix, an invalidation costs real money on the next turn, so `cache_edits` lets you have your eviction without the cache miss. Claude Code calls this "cached microcompact" and runs it every turn for tools on a fixed allowlist (Read, Bash, Grep, Glob, Edit, Write, WebSearch, WebFetch). There's even a sibling time-based variant that wipes stale tool results when the user has been idle for over 60 minutes, since by that point the server-side cache has cold-expired and there's nothing to lose.

OpenClaw can’t do any of this because it doesn’t own the API. What it has instead is a generic `tool-result-context-guard` that shrinks any single tool result over half the context window and throws an overflow error if the total tips past 90%. It's coarser, but it works against any provider. OpenClaw also has explicit "tool-pair repair" code that removes orphaned `tool_result` blocks after history pruning, since Anthropic's API rejects a request with a `tool_result` whose matching `tool_use` has been dropped. Claude Code doesn't need this because its targeted `cache_edits` operations don't leave orphans behind.

Both have sliding-window logic for dropping middle messages when the context starts to swell. Claude Code calls it “snip”, OpenClaw calls it `limitHistoryTurns` and `pruneHistoryForContextShare` (the second one drops oldest until the kept context is at most half the window). Neither is novel. Both are necessary.

The takeaway is that a useful chunk of the work happens before any LLM-based summarisation enters the picture. If you can make the cheap stuff smart enough, you push the expensive call out further and further. Claude Code pushes it furthest because it has API features no one else has access to.

## When eviction isn’t enough

Eventually the cheap stuff runs out and you have to summarise.

Claude Code’s summarisation path is a single LLM call. The whole conversation goes in (with images and documents stripped to placeholders to save tokens for the summariser), and a long structured summary comes out. The output is capped at 20,000 tokens, sized for the p99.99 of historical compact summary lengths (around 17,387 tokens, according to a comment in the code). The call is streamed, so the user sees text appear as it’s generated rather than waiting for a single blocking response. There’s a clever optimisation worth flagging here. The summariser runs through `runForkedAgent`, which inherits the parent agent's prompt cache prefix, so the summarisation call is mostly a cache hit even though it's a brand new request. A comment in `compact.ts` notes that running it without prefix sharing was "98% cache miss, costs ~0.76% of fleet cache\_creation (~38B tok/day)". That's the kind of detail you only see when an agent is running at scale.

OpenClaw’s path is much more elaborate. The summarisation call is delegated to the underlying `pi-coding-agent` SDK, which can use a separate, cheaper model for the compaction call (configured via `agents.defaults.compaction.model`). When the conversation is too big to summarise in one shot, OpenClaw splits it on tool-call boundaries (so an assistant `tool_use` and its matching `tool_result` always stay together), summarises each chunk in a separate LLM call, and then runs a final merge call that stitches the chunk summaries together. The merge prompt is worth quoting in full.

```c
Merge these partial summaries into a single cohesive summary.

MUST PRESERVE:
- Active tasks and their current status (in-progress, blocked, pending)
- Batch operation progress (e.g., '5/17 items completed')
- The last thing the user requested and what was being done about it
- Decisions made and their rationale
- TODOs, open questions, and constraints
- Any commitments or follow-ups promised

PRIORITIZE recent context over older history. The agent needs to know
what it was doing, not just what was discussed.
```

The “5/17 items completed” example is interesting. Whoever wrote this prompt has clearly watched models drop batch progress counters from summaries and learned to call them out specifically. The closing line is the keeper. The agent needs to know what it was doing, not just what was discussed. That’s the entire point of compaction in one sentence.

OpenClaw also has something Claude Code doesn’t, which is a deterministic audit step. After the summary is generated, `auditSummaryQuality` checks that all 5 required section headings appear in order, that every identifier extracted from the last 10 messages survives in the summary text, and that the latest user ask token-overlaps with the summary body. If any of these fail, the summary gets regenerated with structured feedback (`missing_section: ## Decisions`, `missing_identifiers: abc123, ...`). The agent grades its own homework and resubmits.

Claude Code has none of this. It trusts the model to follow the schema on the first try, and it usually does, but there’s no programmatic safety net.

## The prompts tell a story

The summarisation prompts themselves are the clearest window into how each project thinks about compaction.

Claude Code’s main summarisation prompt (`BASE_COMPACT_PROMPT` in `src/services/compact/prompt.ts`) demands a 9-section narrative summary covering primary request and intent, key technical concepts, files and code sections, errors and fixes, problem solving, all user messages (yes, all of them), pending tasks, current work, and an optional next step. The next-step section has an unusual constraint. If there's a next step, the model is told to include "direct quotes from the most recent conversation showing exactly what task you were working on and where you left off. This should be verbatim to ensure there's no drift in task interpretation." This is a clever defence against the well-known model behaviour of subtly drifting away from the user's actual request after summarisation. By forcing verbatim quotes, the post-compaction agent is anchored to the exact words it was working from.

The same prompt opens with an unusually aggressive no-tools preamble. The reason, according to a comment in the same file, is that the cache-sharing fork inherits the parent agent’s full toolset (it has to, otherwise the cache key changes). Sonnet 4.6 with adaptive thinking attempts a tool call during compaction in 2.79% of cases against the older weaker prompt, versus 0.01% on Sonnet 4.5. Adding the explicit “Tool calls will be REJECTED and will waste your only turn, you will fail the task” line at the very top of the prompt was the team’s response to that 4.6 regression. That’s the kind of empirical prompt engineering you only get to do when you have telemetry on millions of agent sessions.

OpenClaw’s prompts are stylistically very different. Where Claude Code reads as a senior engineer writing a careful handoff note, OpenClaw reads as a contract specification the LLM is being asked to implement. The required summary structure is five terse headings (Decisions, Open TODOs, Constraints/Rules, Pending user asks, Exact identifiers). There’s a separate “preserve all opaque identifiers exactly as written, including UUIDs, hashes, IDs, hostnames, IPs, ports, URLs, and file names” instruction with `strict` / `off` / `custom` policy modes, and the audit step actually checks compliance. The aesthetic difference is consistent throughout. Claude Code uses the second person and explanatory prose ("Pay special attention to specific user feedback"). OpenClaw uses imperative blocks ("MUST PRESERVE … PRIORITIZE recent context").

Claude Code’s prompts are warmer and more flexible, which probably helps the model produce a richer narrative. OpenClaw’s prompts are stricter and more verifiable, which means the audit loop has something concrete to check against. The schema choice maps directly to the verification choice.

## Memory as the complement

Both agents have a separate persistent-memory layer that complements compaction. They’ve both decided that the conversation history isn’t the only place state should live. They disagree about how to do it.

Claude Code’s session memory is a single markdown file with 10 fixed sections (Session Title, Current State, Task specification, Files and Functions, Workflow, Errors & Corrections, Codebase and System Documentation, Learnings, Key results, Worklog). The file is updated incrementally throughout the conversation by a forked subagent whose tool permissions are clamped down to “Edit on this one file, nothing else”. Updates fire on a combination of token thresholds (10K to initialise, 5K between updates) and tool-call thresholds (3 calls between updates). When compaction time comes, if the session memory file exists and isn’t empty, it gets injected as the post-compact summary user message and replaces the LLM-driven summarisation entirely. No summary call is made. The persistent file is the summary.

OpenClaw’s memory is an append-only journal of markdown files, indexed in SQLite with FTS5 and embeddings for retrieval. Writes happen during a “memory flush” turn that runs just before compaction, where the same agent (in a tool-restricted mode that only allows append-only writes to one specific path) is asked to extract durable facts. After compaction, the flush isn’t auto-injected back into the context. Instead, the next agent run gets a refresh prompt pointing at the workspace, and the memory contents come back lazily through a memory search tool when needed.

The contrast is structural. Claude Code treats memory as compaction’s substitute, an in-place file with a fixed schema, edited continuously. OpenClaw treats memory as compaction’s preamble, an append-only log indexed for search, written in pre-compaction bursts. Both seem reasonable. The fact that neither converged on the other suggests that the right answer depends heavily on what kind of agent you’re running and how long the sessions tend to be.

## What happens when it breaks

This is where the two systems diverge most starkly.

Claude Code’s recovery model is “circuit-breaker plus lossy fallback”. There are three retry budgets. `MAX_COMPACT_STREAMING_RETRIES` is 2, for transient streaming failures. `MAX_PTL_RETRIES` is 3, for the case where the compaction request itself triggers `prompt_too_long`. When that happens, `truncateHeadForPTLRetry` drops the oldest message groups, prepends a "\[earlier conversation truncated for compaction retry\]" marker, and tries again. It's intentionally lossy. A code comment calls it "the dumb-but-safe fallback for the proactive/manual path". The third budget is `MAX_CONSECUTIVE_AUTOCOMPACT_FAILURES`, set to 3, and the comment that justifies it is the most revealing thing in the entire codebase. "1,279 sessions had 50+ consecutive failures (up to 3,272) in a single session, wasting ~250K API calls/day globally." The circuit breaker exists because somebody at Anthropic looked at the logs and saw real money being burned by agents stuck in compaction loops.

OpenClaw’s recovery model is “snapshot and cancel”. Before pi runs `session.compact()`, OpenClaw copies the on-disk session file to a `.checkpoint.<uuid>.jsonl` sibling. On success, the checkpoint is kept (so the user can roll back). On failure, it's cleaned up. The summarisation safeguard, if it can't produce a clean summary, returns `{ cancel: true }` and lets the underlying SDK continue with the unmodified transcript. The progressive-fallback chain is the cleverest part. `summarizeWithFallback` first tries full summarisation, then falls back to "summarise only the small messages and note the large ones", then to "just note what was there". One OOM chunk doesn't kill the whole compaction.

Both philosophies make sense, but they’re optimising for different things. Claude Code optimises for fast user feedback. If compaction fails, retry silently, fall back to lossy truncation, and if that doesn’t work, give up and stop wasting API calls. OpenClaw optimises for transcript preservation. If compaction can’t be done safely, don’t do it, keep the original conversation, and let the user try again. The Claude Code approach is what you want when the agent is the user’s primary tool and unblocking matters more than fidelity. The OpenClaw approach is what you want when the conversation history is itself a valuable artifact that shouldn’t be silently mutated.

## Cost, latency, and the real tradeoffs

Many discussions of compaction (or any architectural design really) skip the operational dimension entirely. Let’s take a quick numerical look.

On cost, Claude Code is strictly cheaper per fire on the hot path. Three out of four mechanisms (`cachedMicrocompact`, time-based MC, API-side context management) are zero-LLM. The full compact, when it does fire, is cache-warm for the prefix because of the forked-agent trick. There's no cheaper-model option, since the compact uses whatever the main loop is using. OpenClaw can be expensive. The chunked summarisation path issues N+1 LLM calls (one per chunk plus one merge), and each call ships a different chunk as the user message so prefix-cache reuse is essentially zero. To counter that, OpenClaw supports a separate (cheaper) model for the compaction call, which is something Claude Code doesn't.

On latency, Claude Code streams. The user sees output appear incrementally during compaction, and there’s no explicit upper-bound timeout. OpenClaw is sequential. Chunks summarise one after another with plain `await` loops and no `Promise.all` parallelism, so a 4-chunk compaction pays roughly 4× the per-chunk latency plus a merge call. There's a hard 15-minute safety timeout that aborts the whole thing if it stalls.

Claude Code’s compaction stack is cheaper and faster on the hot path because it owns the inference layer and can use API features no one else can. OpenClaw’s stack is more defensive, more verifiable, and more portable, because it has to work across providers it doesn’t control. Both choices follow from where each agent sits in the supply chain.

## Why you should own your harness

Worth pausing on the irony first. Anthropic itself called Claude Code “the thinnest possible wrapper over the model.” Then they shipped a compaction stack with eight distinct mechanisms, a session-memory subsystem updated by a sandboxed forked subagent, a circuit breaker calibrated against production telemetry on stuck sessions, a beta API feature invented specifically so eviction wouldn’t bust the prompt cache, and prompt tuning empirical enough to track stray tool-call rates down to 0.01%. If that’s the thinnest possible wrapper, the thinness is doing a lot of heavy lifting.

So back to the question I opened with. Why not just buy your harness from someone else and treat it as commodity infrastructure?

The comparison answers it pretty clearly. Compaction is one slice of the harness, and on that one slice alone, two of the most popular coding agents on the planet have built stacks that are nearly opposites at every level (reactive versus preemptive, narrative versus structural, memory-replaces-summary versus memory-supplements-summary, lossy-on-failure versus snapshot-on-failure). Both ship to real users every day. Both work. There’s no “right” answer hiding underneath the choices waiting to be discovered.

Think about what that means if you buy your harness off the shelf. You’re inheriting somebody else’s strategies, including whether to let context run hot or estimate every turn, what schema the summary should follow, which side of the unblocking-versus-preservation tradeoff to land on, and what threshold values, retry budgets, and fallback chains to use. None of these are documented in the README, because they’re not features. They’re the invisible scaffolding that determines whether your agent works for your users in your domain, or only for the users and the domain the harness was originally built for. If your agent is a coding assistant for senior engineers and the harness was tuned for novice users on small projects, you’ll feel the mismatch in places you can’t easily debug.

Then there is the calibration question. A comment in the Claude Code codebase justifies a retry budget: “1,279 sessions had 50+ consecutive failures (up to 3,272) in a single session, wasting ~250K API calls per day globally.” That figure did not come from an architectural review, it came from production logs. The same pattern appears in the observation that Sonnet 4.6 attempts unwanted tool calls during compaction 2.79% of the time, compared to 0.01% on Sonnet 4.5. This discrepancy led directly to a rewrite of the no-tools preamble. Again, this was not derived from a prompt engineering guide. It came from operating the agent at scale and measuring its behavior.

Both numbers, and dozens like them in both codebases, are the product of owning the harness end to end and being able to look at your own telemetry. If somebody else owns your harness, they own your telemetry too, and the optimisations they make will be calibrated to their fleet, not yours. You will always be a passenger on someone else’s roadmap.

And compaction is just the visible bit. There’s similar design depth in tool routing, subagent orchestration, the streaming protocol, error classification, cache management, plan persistence, cost accounting. Every one of those layers has the same property. Two reasonable engineers will disagree about it, and the right answer for you depends on your model, your users, your latency budget, and your failure tolerance. You don’t get to opt out of those decisions by buying the harness. You only get to opt out of making them yourself, which is a different and much worse thing.

To be fair, you probably shouldn’t write your harness from scratch on day one. The piece-by-piece reuse that OpenClaw does (wrapping `pi-coding-agent` and supplying the host glue) is a reasonable starting point if you're moving fast. But the moment you start hitting weird production behaviour, or your latency profile diverges from the upstream defaults, or your users care about something the upstream didn't optimise for, you'll be back here reading source code and wishing the boundaries between yours and theirs were a bit more in your favour. The earlier you draw those boundaries with intent, the cheaper this is.

The harness is the vehicle. You’d think pretty hard before buying a vehicle without knowing how the brakes work or what the steering feels like, and the harness is no different. If your agent matters to your business, you should own the part that decides how it behaves under pressure.
