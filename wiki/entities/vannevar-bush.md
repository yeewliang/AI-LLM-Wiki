# Vannevar Bush

## Overview
American engineer and science administrator (1890–1974). Head of the US Office of Scientific Research and Development during WWII, overseeing the Manhattan Project and radar development. After the war, he turned to the long-term question of how humans could manage the exponential growth of scientific knowledge.

## Why this entity matters to AI
Bush's 1945 essay "As We May Think" described the [[memex]] — a personal, associatively-linked knowledge machine. This essay is widely credited as the conceptual origin of hypertext, the World Wide Web, and personal knowledge management. His framing of associative indexing as superior to hierarchical indexing anticipates the graph-based knowledge structures that underlie modern tools like [[obsidian]] and the [[llm-wiki-pattern]].

His central problem statement — that knowledge is accumulating faster than humans can manage it, and that the bottleneck is not collection but organisation and retrieval — is precisely the problem the [[llm-wiki-pattern]] addresses 80 years later.

## Key works / outputs
- "As We May Think" (1945, *The Atlantic*) — defines the [[memex]]; coins "associative trails"; influenced Ted Nelson, Douglas Engelbart, Tim Berners-Lee
- *Science: The Endless Frontier* (1945) — policy document that shaped US federal research funding and created the National Science Foundation

## Affiliations and relationships
- MIT — Dean of Engineering
- Carnegie Institution of Washington — President
- US Office of Scientific Research and Development — Director (WWII)

## Current status / latest developments
Bush died in 1974. "As We May Think" (1945) is still widely cited in HCI, knowledge management, and AI literature. The [[llm-wiki-pattern]] is frequently described as the closest practical realisation of his [[memex]] vision to date.
